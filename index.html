Add the tone

Write in a direct, conversational tone. No jargon. Short sentences. Do not use: em dashes, 'it is worth noting,' 'in conclusion,' 'leverage,' 'the goal is simple,' 'it is not X it is Y,' 'at the end of the day,' 'it is worth mentioning,' 'in today's world,' 'this is a reminder that,' or 'as we continue to.' If you catch yourself writing any of these, delete the sentence and start over.

Part 2: Make it not look like AI
AI writing has patterns. Once you see them you cannot unsee them. These are the five things that give it away every time.

The five tells

Tell 1 of 5
Em dashes everywhere.
"This is important — in fact — it changes everything."
AI defaults to em dashes to add drama. They just add clutter. Replace with a period or rewrite the sentence.
Tell 2 of 5
Filler openers.
"It is worth noting that our team has made significant progress."
Delete everything before the actual point. "Our team made significant progress." Done.
Tell 3 of 5
Three-point lists. Always exactly three.
"There are three key areas: alignment, communication, and growth."
AI loves the rule of three. If you have two things, say two. If you have four, say four.
Tell 4 of 5
Vague language. No real details.
"Our team has seen strong results across multiple initiatives this quarter."
Which results? Which initiatives? Replace every vague claim with a real number or fact only you would know.
Tell 5 of 5
No voice. Could be from any company.
"The goal is not the output. It is about cultivating a mindset of continuous improvement."
If another company could publish this exact sentence, delete it. Add your actual perspective.

🌟 Start, Stop, Continue: A Framework for Growth

It is worth noting that the Start, Stop, Continue framework — when applied thoughtfully — is one of the most powerful self-reflection tools available to professionals today. In today's world, personal development is not just encouraged — it is expected. This serves as a reminder that intentional growth does not happen by accident.

Key areas of focus include:

  • Clarity: Understanding which behaviors drive results and committing to them going forward
  • Accountability: Owning our outcomes rather than attributing them to external factors
  • Alignment: Ensuring personal goals stay connected to the broader organizational vision

As we continue to evolve as an organization, it is worth mentioning that this exercise is not simply about filling out a template — it is about cultivating a mindset of continuous improvement. At the end of the day, the goal is not to list actions, but to demonstrate a commitment to real change. Our teams have seen strong results across a number of key initiatives this quarter, and this framework positions us to build on that momentum going forward.

In conclusion, growth is not linear. The Start, Stop, Continue approach — when leveraged effectively — becomes a catalyst for both individual and team-level transformation. This is the kind of intentional work that moves organizations forward.
