Without this, Gemini writes like a consultant who's billing by the word. Copy this. Paste it into every session.

Give it a tone

Write in a direct, conversational tone. No jargon. Short sentences. Do not use: em dashes, 'it is worth noting,' 'in conclusion,' 'leverage,' 'the goal is simple,' 'it is not X it is Y,' 'at the end of the day,' 'it is worth mentioning,' 'in today's world,' 'this is a reminder that,' or 'as we continue to.' If you catch yourself writing any of these, delete the sentence and start over.

Part 2: Make it not look like AI
AI writing has patterns. Once you see them you cannot unsee them. These are the five things that give it away every time.

The five tells

1. Em dashes everywhere.
"This is important — and here is why — it changes everything."
2. Filler openers.
"It is worth noting that our team has made significant progress."
3. Everything in exactly three points. Always.
"There are three key areas: alignment, communication, and growth."
4. No specific details.
"Our team has seen strong results across multiple initiatives this quarter."
5. No voice.
"The goal is simple — it is not about the output, it is about the process."
This was written by Gemini. Tap every phrase that gives it away. All of them. You cannot move on until you find them all.

0 of 8 found.

It is worth noting that the Start, Stop, Continue framework — when applied thoughtfully — represents a significant opportunity for personal and professional growth. There are three key areas: clarity, accountability, and reflection. At the end of the day, the goal is not simply to list actions, but to demonstrate a commitment to continuous improvement.

As we continue to evolve as a team, it is worth mentioning that this exercise serves as a reminder that growth is not linear. The goal is simple — it is not about perfection, it is about progress. Our people have shown strong results across a number of initiatives, and this deck is an opportunity to celebrate that momentum going forward.

Three edits. That's all it takes to make this sound like a person wrote it.

The three-edit fix

1. Delete filler openers
Before
"It is worth noting that the framework is useful."
After
"This framework works."
2. Replace em dashes with a period or nothing
Before
"Growth — when done right — takes time."
After
"Growth takes time when done right."
3. Add one detail only you would know
Before
"Our team has seen strong results."
After
"Since January, our People Team has cut onboarding time by two weeks."
These two prompts do most of the work. Save them somewhere you will actually find them.
